Legal Aspects of Online Privacy

Big Tech

Some Big Tech companies have recently come under fire for collecting more information from users than they are aware of. For example, Cambridge Analytica secretly analyzed Facebook user’s information to create psychological profiles on them. They did not disclose that they were virtually data mining and using people’s information without explicit consent. Although Facebook did not directly encourage this behavior, data breaches allowed for user’s privacy to be invaded.

The Fourth Amendment

The fourth amendment states that:
“The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.”

Although no specific law on data collection is in place, it doesn’t take away from the fact people have a right to their own property and privacy, which should not be invaded without appropriate legal waivers. Many tech companies toe the line of doing so by collecting user data with the excuse that they are making the browsing experience more convenient for their users. The reality of the matter, however, is that with so much data collected on users habits, big tech companies are selling big chunks of their collections for a lot of money, profiting off their users personal information.
